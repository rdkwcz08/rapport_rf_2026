\section{Task 3.2 --- One-Diode Topology with Microstrip Lines}
\label{sec:task3_2}

This section repeats steps~(4) and~(5) from Task~3.1, progressively
replacing the lumped components of the matching network with microstrip
lines.
The goal is to move closer to a circuit that can realistically be
implemented on a PCB, where the physical traces play an active role in
impedance matching, and to compare the results with those obtained using
the purely lumped circuit.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Step 1 --- Accounting for Physical Traces
            Using MLIN and MTEE Blocks}
\label{subsec:mlin_mtee}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Principle}

In a real PCB circuit, every component is connected to the rest of the
circuit through copper traces.
These traces have a defined length, width, and substrate, which gives them
their own electrical properties (distributed inductance and capacitance):
they therefore cannot be treated as ideal wires.

To account for this in ADS, each component of the matching network
(capacitor and inductor) is surrounded by \texttt{MLIN} (Microstrip Line)
blocks, which model physical microstrip line segments.
When a node in the schematic connects three branches together (for example,
the junction between the main line, the shunt component, and the rest of
the circuit), an \texttt{MTEE} block is used instead of a simple wire
connection.
This T-shaped component models the physical discontinuity that such a
junction creates on a PCB, and accounts for the junction effects that can
alter the circuit behavior at high frequency.

    \caption{ADS schematic of the matching network with \texttt{MLIN}
             and \texttt{MTEE} blocks surrounding the lumped components.

\subsubsection{Resonance Frequency Shift}

When the simulation is run using the matching network values from Task~3.1
and the $S_{11}$ is observed, the resonance dip is no longer centered at
\SI{2.2}{\giga\hertz}.
The physical traces introduce additional reactive effects that shift the
matching frequency.

A new tuning step is therefore required to bring the dip back to
\SI{2.2}{\giga\hertz}, by adjusting the capacitance, inductance, and
line length values.
After tuning, the optimal values obtained are:
%
\begin{equation}
    L_\mathrm{line} = \SI{8.025}{\milli\meter}, \quad
    C = \SI{23.552}{\pico\farad}, \quad
    L_\mathrm{ind} = \SI{9.38}{\nano\henry}.
    \label{eq:retuning_mlin}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Simplification to a 1C--1L Topology}

To simplify the schematic and reduce the number of components on the PCB,
the matching network is reduced to a single shunt capacitor and a single
series inductor.
A parameter sweep is performed in ADS over the three variables
($L_\mathrm{line}$, $C$ and $L_\mathrm{ind}$) to find the values that
center the $S_{11}$ dip at \SI{2.2}{\giga\hertz}.
The optimal values obtained from this sweep are:
%
\begin{equation}
    L_\mathrm{line} = \SI{4.19}{\milli\meter}, \quad
    C = \SI{39}{\pico\farad}, \quad
    L_\mathrm{ind} = \SI{2.7}{\nano\henry}.
    \label{eq:opt_1c1l}
\end{equation}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Step 2 --- Replacing the Capacitor and Inductor
            with Microstrip Lines}
\label{subsec:full_ms}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Principle}

In this second step, the approach is taken further: the lumped capacitor
and inductor are entirely removed and each replaced by an \texttt{MLIN}
block whose width~$W$ and length~$L$ are adjusted.

The key idea is that a narrow microstrip line behaves as a distributed
inductor, while a wide microstrip line behaves as a distributed capacitor.
By tuning these two geometric parameters, it is therefore possible to
reproduce the effect of the lumped capacitor and inductor without needing
any additional SMD components.

This approach is advantageous for PCB manufacturing, as it reduces the
number of components to be soldered and eliminates performance variations
caused by component tolerances.

\subsubsection{Design Rules}

The constraints imposed for this step are as follows:
%
\begin{itemize}
    \item Only the width~$W$ and length~$L$ of the two \texttt{MLIN} blocks
          replacing the capacitor and inductor may be adjusted;
    \item $W < \SI{4}{\milli\meter}$ for each of the two lines;
    \item $L < \SI{20}{\milli\meter}$ for each of the two lines.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Results after Tuning}

Tuning in ADS over the four geometric parameters
($W_C$, $L_C$, $W_L$, $L_L$) yields an impedance match centered at
\SI{2.2}{\giga\hertz}.
The optimal values are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:ms_final_dims}.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Optimized dimensions of the two microstrip lines replacing
             the capacitor and inductor (one-diode topology,
             \SI{2.2}{\giga\hertz}).}
    \label{tab:ms_final_dims}
    \begin{tabular}{llS[table-format=1.2]l}
        \toprule
        Replaced component & Parameter
            & {Optimized value} & Unit \\
        \midrule
        Capacitor  & Width  $W_C$ & 0.56 & mm \\
                   & Length $L_C$ & 1.8  & mm \\
        \midrule
        Inductor   & Width  $W_L$ & 1.32 & mm \\
                   & Length $L_L$ & 7.6  & mm \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

It can be noted that the line replacing the capacitor
($W_C = \SI{0.56}{\milli\meter}$) is narrower than the line replacing the
inductor ($W_L = \SI{1.32}{\milli\meter}$).
This is consistent with the expected behavior: a narrow line has a high
characteristic impedance (inductive behavior), while a wide line has a low
characteristic impedance (capacitive behavior).
The lengths allow fine-tuning of the equivalent reactance at the target
frequency.

\paragraph{Analysis.}
Both circuits achieve satisfactory impedance matching at
\SI{2.2}{\giga\hertz} ($S_{11} < \SI{-15}{\deci\bel}$), but their
behaviors differ slightly.
The microstrip topology achieves a deeper $S_{11}$ dip, since the line
dimensions allow continuous tuning of the reactance, unlike the discrete
values of SMD components.
On the other hand, the matching bandwidth tends to be narrower, making
the circuit more sensitive to frequency drift.
For an RF energy harvesting application targeting the ISM band at
\SI{2.2}{\giga\hertz}, this narrower bandwidth is acceptable, since the
RF source (Wi-Fi, cellular network) is a nearly single-frequency carrier.
